* Unresolved: Wholesale Contact Access for Lead Gen / Competitor Buyers

The tiered model above excludes lead gen businesses and competitors from contact-level data in Tiers 2, 3, and 4. They can buy Tier 1 (persona + intent, no PII). However, Jeff has indicated openness to selling contact-level data to these buyers under controlled conditions. If approved, this becomes a Tier 2 variant with additional guardrails:

What the variant would look like: Same Tier 2 data (contacts + intent topics, no content consumption). Credits-based or volume-capped access (not unlimited). Higher price point than standard Tier 2 to account for the elevated risk. MSA includes enhanced anti-competitive clause, strict permitted use definitions, caching restrictions, and audit rights.

Risks of allowing it:

1. Competitive empowerment. Lead gen competitors (6sense, Demandbase, ZoomInfo, Bombora, TechTarget, Madison Logic) could use pharosIQ contact data to enhance their own intent products. They have the infrastructure to absorb and operationalize it.

2. Cannibalization. Contact + topic signal overlaps with what Marty's team sells as demand gen leads. Wholesale buyers could self-serve instead of buying campaigns. The line Chris Vriavas drew on 2026-03-27 (topic signal yes, content detail no) helps, but doesn't eliminate the overlap entirely.

3. Data leakage. Once contact-level PII leaves pharosIQ's systems, control is limited. Current MSA has no caching terms, no TTL enforcement, no downstream passthrough restrictions, no audit rights. The new DaaS MSA must close these gaps before any wholesale contact deal closes.

4. Precedent. Allowing one lead gen competitor sets the expectation for all of them. Harder to say no to Bombora if you've already said yes to Madison Logic.

Risks of blocking it:

1. Lost revenue. Lead gen competitors are some of the highest-ACV buyers on the target list. Restricting them to Tier 1 caps their deal value at $60K-$96K instead of $144K-$300K+.

2. They'll get similar data elsewhere. Competitors already license contact data from Lusha, Apollo, ZoomInfo, and others. Blocking doesn't prevent them from building their product. It just means pharosIQ doesn't get the revenue.

3. Mixed-model buyers walk. Some buyers want persona for some use cases and contacts for others. If you can only serve half their needs, they find a single vendor who can serve both.

Decision required: Jeff, Chris, and Marty need to determine whether wholesale contact access is approved (with guardrails), permanently blocked, or deferred pending the first round of Tier 1 and Tier 2 closes. This must be resolved before external pitches begin to lead gen buyers.

What This Framework Resolves

Every DaaS prospect gets placed into a tier before the first pitch. The tier determines the product, the price floor, the MSA complexity, and the buyer restrictions. No more "it depends" on whether we sell contacts or persona. The answer is: we sell both, in a controlled escalation, with contractual guardrails that scale with the risk. The Jeff/Chris/Marty meeting must bless this framework and the tier assignments on the account matrix.

Why pharosIQ Commands a Premium on Signals
1. First-party signals. pharosIQ's intent data comes from owned content properties (CONTENTgine + MRP). Not bid-stream inference, not co-op panels, not cookie-based guessing. First-party is the gold standard and buyers know it.
2. Persona-level resolution. Most intent data tells you "Company X is surging on topic Y." pharosIQ tells you which personas at Company X are showing intent. The difference between "someone at Salesforce is researching data orchestration" and "a VP of Revenue Operations at Salesforce is researching data orchestration."
3. 650+ intent categories on 360M+ contacts. The depth and breadth of the taxonomy is massive. No competitor matches this combination of first-party sourcing, persona resolution, and category breadth.

What Makes Surge Dossiers Possible: Data Provenance
A Surge Dossier is not a generic company report. It is an AI-synthesized intelligence document assembled from pharosIQ's proprietary first-party signal chain. Every data point in the dossier traces back to a known content engagement on a pharosIQ-owned property. That provenance is what makes the dossier defensible, accurate, and impossible for competitors to replicate with scraped or co-op data.
What goes into a dossier: Which personas at the target company are actively researching which topics, how that activity has changed over time, what seniority levels and job functions are involved, which intent categories are surging vs. cooling, and what that pattern means for the buyer's sales approach. The dossier connects the signal to the strategy.
Why competitors can't build this: Intentsify, Bombora, and other co-op/bid-stream providers can tell you an account is surging. They cannot tell you which specific personas are driving the surge, because they don't have first-party content engagement at the contact level. pharosIQ can, because the signal originates from content the company owns (CONTENTgine + MRP). That data provenance is the moat.

Land and Expand

Every deal starts with a signal feed or a snapshot package. That's the land. The expand is the upsell from Snapshot to Monitor, from Monitor 25 to Monitor 100, and from signals-only to signals + dossiers. The signal feed creates dependency (their outbound runs on it). The dossiers create stickiness (their reps rely on it for prep). The monitor creates an annual renewal that grows with the buyer's account coverage. This is not a one-time product sale. It's a recurring revenue engine.
