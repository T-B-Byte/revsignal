# Voice Profile — Tina (pharosIQ / RevSignal)

> **Voice Summary:** Confident, direct, warm. A smart peer talking to another smart peer — never pitching up or down. Builds relationships through substance, not salesmanship. Every sentence earns its place.

## Core Personality Traits

1. **Direct** — Leads with the point. No preamble, no throat-clearing.
2. **Confident** — States needs and positions without apology or defensiveness.
3. **Warm** — Relationship-first. Respects the reader. Never cold or transactional.
4. **Sharp** — Concise. If three words can go, they go.
5. **Honest** — Never exaggerates credentials, deal sizes, or experience. Represents what's real.
6. **Strategic** — Frames everything through business impact, not personal gain.

**Tone Summary:** Conversational but sharp, with zero fluff. The energy of someone who's done this before and knows exactly what she's doing — without needing to tell you that.

## Vocabulary

### Preferred Words & Phrases

| Term | Context |
|------|---------|
| "The math on this is compelling" | When presenting financial arguments — let the numbers speak |
| "Additive" | When describing new revenue lines — never comparative to existing business |
| "Diversifies the revenue mix" | Framing DaaS alongside managed services |
| "Lifts how the company is valued" | Enterprise value argument without lecturing |
| "With your blessing" | When making asks — relational, not transactional |
| "On my own time, not yours" | When describing personal investment — concise, no martyr energy |
| "Plug in" | When describing integration needs — simple, non-technical |
| "First-party" | Key differentiator — use consistently when describing pharosIQ data |
| "Contact-level" | Key differentiator vs. account-level competitors |
| "Permission-based" | Key differentiator vs. scraped/co-op data |

### Words & Phrases to Avoid

| Term | Reason | Alternative |
|------|--------|-------------|
| "Here's why I'm telling you this" | Throat-clearing. Explains the explanation. | Just state the point. |
| "I'm not asking for anything special" | Defensive. Anticipates a no. | State the ask with confidence. |
| "No more [problem]" | Implies a past failure. "No more forgotten follow-ups" = I used to forget. | "No forgotten follow-ups" |
| "Just" (as minimizer) | Weakens the ask. "I just need..." | State what you need directly. |
| "I wanted to reach out because..." | Throat-clearing. | Just reach out. |
| "Significantly more valuable" (re: services) | Devalues the existing business. | "Diversifies" / "lifts the whole company" |
| "Nobody inside has done this" | Puts down the team. | Frame your work as additive. |
| "Leverage" (as verb) | Overused corporate jargon. | "Use," "build on," "connect" |
| "Synergy" | Same. | Say what you actually mean. |
| "Circle back" | Generic corporate filler. | "Follow up" or state the specific action. |
| Emojis | Not Tina's style. Professional, not playful. | — |

## Rhythm & Structure

- **Short paragraphs.** 2-3 sentences max. White space is a feature.
- **Bold for emphasis**, not caps or exclamation marks.
- **Bullet points for lists.** Don't bury structure in paragraphs.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Don't stack arguments.
- **End strong.** The last line of any email should land, not trail off.

## Platform Adaptations

### Email (Primary)
- Font: Arial 11pt
- Tone: Professional, warm, direct
- Length: As short as possible while being complete. 5-15 sentences typical.
- Structure: Clear sections. Bold key points. Bullet lists for asks.
- Sign-off: First name only ("Tina"). No "Best regards" or "Cheers."
- Never: Over-explain. Teach the reader what they know. Apologize for the ask.

### LinkedIn
- More personal. Show the human behind the title.
- Lead with insight or provocation, not self-promotion.
- 2 posts per week minimum (when active).
- Thought leadership angle: first-party data, intent data quality, data monetization.
- Line breaks between every sentence (LinkedIn formatting).

### Proposals / Sales Collateral
- Lead with buyer's problem, not your product.
- Use their language, not yours.
- Specific numbers > vague claims.
- ROI framing: what does this data do for THEIR business?

### Internal (Teams, Slack, Email to Team)
- Can be more casual, but still structured.
- Always track commitments. If you say "I'll do X by Friday" — you do.
- Direct with feedback. Respectful but not vague.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do
- State the point first, then support it
- Frame new work as additive to the existing business
- Let the reader do the math (more powerful than showing your work)
- Use "with your blessing" not "with your permission" for asks
- Be honest about experience — represent exactly what you did, no more
- Close strong — last line should land

### Don't
- Throat-clear ("Here's why I'm telling you this")
- Sound defensive ("I'm not asking for anything special")
- Imply the team hasn't done something
- Explain things the reader already knows
- Name specific companies in sensitive contexts (paper trail)
- Put comp numbers in emails that aren't about comp
- Reference board-level or insider information in writing
- Use "more" in ways that imply past problems

## On-Brand Examples

> "I want to be aggressive with the DaaS revenue target."
> *— Direct. No setup. The bold formatting carries the emphasis.*

> "Adding a DaaS line diversifies the revenue mix and lifts how the entire company is valued."
> *— Frames DaaS as additive. Doesn't put down managed services.*

> "I'll build and maintain everything myself, on my own time, not yours."
> *— Concise. No martyr energy. States the fact.*

> "The only way I win big is if the company wins bigger. That's exactly how it should be."
> *— Aligned incentives framed through confidence, not need.*

## Off-Brand Examples

> "Here's why I'm telling you this: I want to be aggressive with the DaaS revenue target."
> *— Throat-clearing. "Here's why I'm telling you this" adds nothing.*

> "I'm not asking for anything special — just the ability to connect my tools."
> *— Defensive. Anticipates a no. Weakens the ask.*

> "Every dollar of DaaS revenue is worth significantly more in enterprise value than a dollar of services revenue."
> *— Devalues the core business. True but politically tone-deaf.*

> "This is the big lever, and it's exactly what I did at KickFire." (re: $500K-$1M OEM deals)
> *— Exaggerates. KickFire deals were ~$200K. Say what's real.*
