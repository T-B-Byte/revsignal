## 1. WHO IS PHAROSIQ

B2B intent data company formed from the merger of MRP + CONTENTgine (March 2024). ~$60M revenue, $11M profit, no debt, ~1,373 employees. CEO Jeff Rokuskie wants to sell the company this year, so speed-to-revenue and valuation multiples matter.

**The data asset:**
- 360M+ verified business contacts (updated March 2026)
- 50M B2B inboxable contacts
- 1.6M actively engaged contacts in any 90-day window
- 25M+ global companies covered
- 650+ proprietary intent categories
- 3-6M new signals generated monthly
- Intent scoring: 1-99 scale weighted by content type, buyer seniority, recency, momentum

**Why the data is different:**
- **First-party sourced** from owned content properties (CONTENTgine + MRP newsletters, 500K+ curated content assets). Not bid-stream inference. Not co-op panels. Not scraped.
- **Permission-based engagement** with documented provenance
- **Persona-level resolution**: identifies WHO (title, seniority, function) is researching WHAT (topic), not just which COMPANY is surging
- **90-day signal decay window** so signals reflect current behavior, not stale history

The Director of Marketing Analytics who attended a webinar on attribution modeling. You know who they are, what they care about, and when they engaged.

## 3. FILLER STORIES: THE PAIN OF ACCOUNT-LEVEL INTENT

Use these as relatable anecdotes during the demo to make the data feel real.

### Story 1: "The Monday Morning Surge List"
> Every Monday, marketing ops downloads the Bombora surge report. 500 accounts surging on their intent topics. They hand it to sales. Sales opens the first account: "Acme Corp is surging on Cloud Security."
>
> Great. Acme Corp has 12,000 employees. Who do they call?
>
> Sales searches LinkedIn. Finds a CISO. Emails them. No response. Because the CISO isn't the one researching. It's a "Senior Manager, Security Architecture" buried three levels deep, a title that doesn't show up in standard searches, who's been consuming whitepapers on zero-trust architecture for the last three weeks.
>
> The signal was perfect. The activation failed because they couldn't see the person behind the account.

### Story 2: "The $100K Blind Spot"
> A mid-market SaaS company spends $100K/quarter on LinkedIn ads targeting "VP of Marketing" and "Director of Demand Gen." Their campaigns perform fine. CPLs are around $100.
>
> What they don't know: LinkedIn only recognizes 55% of the job titles on its own platform. "Head of Growth Marketing," "VP Revenue Marketing," "Senior Director, Integrated Campaigns," and 200 other variations of the same buyer are completely invisible to their targeting.
>
> They're paying full price to reach half their audience. The other half isn't being reached by a competitor. It's not being reached by anyone. It's just invisible.

### Story 3: "The 13-Person Buying Committee"
> The average B2B buying committee now has 11-13 stakeholders. Your ABM platform identifies the account as in-market. Your CRM has 3 contacts at that account: the VP of IT, a procurement manager, and someone who visited the booth at a conference two years ago.
>
> The actual buying committee includes 10 other people you've never heard of. The "Director of Enterprise Architecture" who's been evaluating vendors for 6 weeks. The "Head of Digital Transformation" who owns the budget. The "VP of Business Applications" who has veto power.
>
> You're running a 13-person committee play with 3 contacts. That's not ABM. That's a coin flip.

### Story 4: "The Integration Tax"
> A demand gen agency manages 20 client campaigns. Each client has their own intent data provider. Some use Bombora. Some use 6sense. One uses G2. Three don't use intent data at all.
>
> The agency's ops team spends 15 hours per week normalizing account-level surge lists from four different providers into a format their activation platforms can use. And even after all that work, the output is the same: a list of companies, with no people attached.
>
> What if every client campaign had the same underlying signal: first-party, persona-level intent, with verified contacts, from a single source? That's not a data feed. That's an infrastructure play that makes every campaign smarter from day one.

### Story 5: "The Decay Problem"
> You built a target audience list in January. 10,000 contacts. By April, 30% of the job titles have changed. People got promoted, moved companies, changed roles. Your "VP of Marketing" is now a "Chief Revenue Officer" at a different company, and a "Senior Marketing Manager" just got promoted into the role you're targeting, but your system doesn't know about her yet.
>
> B2B contact data decays 30%+ per year. Titles change faster than emails. Static lists die within 90 days.
>
> Intent signals on a rolling 90-day window solve this. You're not targeting a list. You're targeting a behavior. The people who show up are the people who are actively researching right now, regardless of what their title was when you first built the list.

## 4. FILLER STORIES: THE MAGNITUDE OF THE DATA

Use these to convey scale and strategic value.

### Story 1: "360 Million Isn't a Database. It's a Census."
> There are roughly 800 million professionals on LinkedIn. pharosIQ has verified contact records for 360M+ of them. Not modeled. Not inferred. Records generated from real content engagement across owned properties.
>
> In any 90-day window, 1.6 million of those contacts are actively engaging with content across 650+ intent categories. That's not a static database you query. It's a living signal layer that refreshes continuously.
>
> When you ask "who at Acme Corp is researching data analytics?", the answer comes from actual content consumption, not from a propensity model that guesses based on firmographics.

### Story 2: "650 Categories Is Not Granularity for Granularity's Sake"
> Bombora has ~8,000 topics, but they're built from bid-stream and co-op signals, so the specificity is diluted by noise. pharosIQ's 650+ categories are built from first-party content consumption. Each category maps to real content assets that real people consumed.
>
> The difference: when pharosIQ says "VP of IT at Acme Corp is surging on Zero Trust Architecture," it means that person downloaded a whitepaper, attended a webinar, or engaged with editorial content specifically about zero trust. Not that someone at Acme Corp's IP address visited a webpage that mentioned security.
>
> First-party signals are narrower in raw count but deeper in meaning. That's the trade-off. And for activation, depth beats breadth.

### Story 3: "The Persona Resolution Advantage"
> Account-level intent: "Acme Corp is surging on Cloud Migration."
> Persona-level intent: "A VP of Infrastructure at Acme Corp (enterprise, manufacturing, NAMER) has been consuming content on hybrid cloud architecture and containerization for the past 21 days."
>
> The first one tells marketing to add Acme Corp to a campaign. The second one tells sales exactly who to call, what to talk about, and how urgent it is.
>
> This isn't a marginal improvement. It's a category shift. Account-level intent creates awareness. Persona-level intent creates pipeline.

### Story 4: "First-Party vs. Co-Op: The Exclusivity Problem"
> Co-op intent data (Bombora's model) is shared across all members of the co-op. When Bombora says "Acme Corp is surging," every Bombora customer sees the same signal. Your competitors are reaching out to the same accounts, the same week, with the same trigger.
>
> First-party intent data from pharosIQ is exclusive to the buyer. The signals come from content properties that pharosIQ owns. There is no co-op. There is no sharing. When you see a surge, your competitors don't, unless they're also pharosIQ customers (and even then, contractual exclusivity can be negotiated).
>
> In a world where every ABM team is running the same Bombora playbook, exclusivity isn't a feature. It's the entire competitive advantage.

### Story 5: "The Signal-to-Noise Ratio"
> A typical Bombora surge report shows 500 accounts surging on your topics in a given week. The signal is real but thin: an IP-based match that says someone at the company's office visited a website associated with the topic.
>
> A pharosIQ signal: a named individual, with a verified title, at a confirmed company, who consumed specific content, on a specific date, from a first-party source. That signal has a name, a face, a topic, a timestamp, and a provenance trail.
>
> When you stack 500 anonymous account surges against 50 verified persona signals, the 50 win every time. Because your SDR can actually do something with them.

## 5. HOW THEY CAN USE THE DATA STRATEGICALLY

### Use Case 1: CRM/CDP Enrichment
> Layer pharosIQ's persona-level intent signals directly into your CRM or CDP. Every contact record gets enriched with: what topics they're researching, when they last engaged, their seniority and function match to your ICP, and a recency-weighted intent score. This turns your CRM from a static contact database into a dynamic prioritization engine. Sales doesn't work the list alphabetically. They work it by signal strength.

### Use Case 2: Lead Scoring Transformation
> Most lead scoring models use firmographic fit + behavioral engagement (website visits, email opens). Add persona-level intent as a third dimension: is this contact at a company that's actively researching your category? Is this specific person the one doing the research? A "Director of IT" who opened your email AND is surging on your intent topic is a fundamentally different lead than one who just opened the email.

### Use Case 3: Buying Committee Mapping
> For any target account, pharosIQ can surface every contact who's engaged with content in your category. Not just the one contact in your CRM, but the full buying committee: the economic buyer, the technical evaluator, the champion, the blocker. With 360M+ contacts and persona-level engagement data, you see the committee that account-level data can't reveal.

### Use Case 4: Campaign Prioritization
> Stop spreading ad budget evenly across your target account list. Use intent signals to identify the 15-25% of accounts that are actively in-market right now, then concentrate 60-80% of spend on those accounts. Industry benchmarks show 2-3x conversion rates on intent-qualified accounts vs. cold. The math is simple: same budget, 2-3x more pipeline.

### Use Case 5: Competitive Displacement Timing
> pharosIQ tracks 650+ intent categories, including competitor-specific topics. When a target account surges on "Bombora alternatives" or "6sense evaluation," that's a displacement signal. Your SDR doesn't need to cold-call. They reach out when the buyer is already looking to switch.

### Use Case 6: Renewal Risk and Expansion Signals
> For existing customers, intent signals can flag both risk and opportunity. If a customer starts surging on competitor topics, that's a churn signal. If they start surging on adjacent categories, that's an expansion signal. Both are invisible without persona-level intent data.
